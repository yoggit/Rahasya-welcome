The Logic of Locks

Real cryptography for curious minds — build every cipher, then break it. Spin a Caesar wheel to hide a message, then play the eavesdropper who cracks it in 25 guesses.

Rahasya — meaning secret — teaches the real standard algorithms, not invented toys. Each rung earns the next: a lock, then why it fails, then the lock that fixes it. The attacker is first-class, because you must think like one to build a good lock.

How a rung works

Every cipher is taught the same honest way — make it, attack it, then see why the next one exists.

1

Build it

You're the sender. Type a message, turn the key, and watch the cipher scramble it live — every letter, as it happens.

2

Break it

Now you're Eve, the eavesdropper. Attack the very lock you just built, with the real tool that beats it.

3

Learn why

What just failed, and what fixes it — which hands you the next rung. No lock is presented as safe when it isn't.

Inside rung 1

What a child actually does on the rung that's live today.

🎡

Turn the wheel

A rotating cipher disc. Type a message, pick a shift, and see the ciphertext form letter by letter.

🔨

Crack it yourself, then let the computer

You play Eve: turn a dial to hunt the shift by hand — a coach walks you through it. Only then does the "try all 25 at once" button unlock, so you feel why 25 keys is no protection.

🧠

Meet Kerckhoffs

Eve knew the method and broke it anyway. Only the key should need to be secret — the rule every real cipher obeys.
